## Governance Mechanics

The **district layer** is where community experiments scale to policy affecting tens of thousands of people. Here's how it operates:

**District Sovereignty:**
- Lincoln School District can set food sourcing policies within federal/state nutrition guidelines
- Elected school board makes policy decisions on behalf of district constituents
- District can allocate budget to local priorities (food, programs, staff)
- Must comply with state education law and federal school meal requirements

**Constitutional Constraints:**
- Must meet federal nutrition standards (USDA requirements)
- Cannot discriminate in meal access (all students entitled to meals)
- Subject to state health and safety regulations
- Must maintain fiscal responsibility and transparency
- Cannot override family sovereignty (families can opt out of meal program)

**How District Authority Works:**

1. **Policy-Making (elected governance):**
   - School board is elected by district residents every 3-4 years
   - Board sets policy direction (local food integration approved here)
   - Superintendent implements board decisions
   - Community input through public comment process (Sarah's testimony)

2. **Resource Allocation (public budget):**
   - District food budget: $2.3 million/year (public funds)
   - Board decides how to spend: corporate contracts vs. local sourcing
   - New allocation: $240K/year for hub coordinators + infrastructure
   - Transparent budgeting (public meetings, published reports)

3. **Institutional Partnerships (scaling community work):**
   - Community hubs operate independently (community layer)
   - District contracts with hubs for services (district-community partnership)
   - Hub coordinators become district partners (professionalized care work)
   - Mutual accountability (hubs deliver services, district provides resources)

4. **Cross-Neighborhood Coordination (district-wide patterns):**
   - District sees patterns across all 18 schools
   - Identifies successful interventions (community hubs work)
   - Scales what works (food hub model â†’ district policy)
   - Resources what individuals/communities can't afford (commercial kitchens, guaranteed contracts)

**Subsidiarity in Action:**

*Why this happens at district layer, not higher or lower:*

- **Not Community Layer:** Multiple communities across district need coordination; too big for single community to address
- **Not Municipal Layer:** School districts often don't align with city boundaries; district has authority over school food policy
- **Not State Layer:** Local food sourcing is district decision within state guidelines; no need for state-wide mandate

*What can be decided at district layer:*
- School meal program sourcing and vendors
- District budget allocation for food infrastructure
- Partnerships with community organizations
- Educational curriculum integration (farm visits, nutrition education)
- Facility use (schools' kitchens available for community hubs on weekends)

*What cannot be decided at district layer:*
- Federal nutrition standards (set by USDA)
- State health regulations (set by state health department)
- Individual family meal choices (family layer)
- Municipal zoning for farm stands (municipal layer)
